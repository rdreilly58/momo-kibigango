## 🎯 Introduction Strategy

**Core Message (all versions):**
- Who you are (background, credentials)
- What you care about (technical excellence, people, delivery, AI responsibility)
- What you need from them (help, input, partnership)
- Tone: Curious, collaborative, humble, confident

**Key Principles:**
1. Lead with warmth and curiosity, not authority
2. Acknowledge what came before ("building on what you've created")
3. Be specific about your background (credibility without chest-thumping)
4. Ask for input ("I need your help")
5. Signal long-term commitment ("excited to be here")

---

## 30-Second Version
### (Elevator pitch, hallway conversation, quick intro)

**Time:** ~30 seconds | **Tone:** Warm, energetic, grounded

---

**"Hi, I'm Robert Reilly. I just joined as Team Lead here. I come from [background/previous role], where I focused on building high-performing teams and delivering reliable systems. I'm excited to be here because Leidos is doing critical work, and I believe great engineering—solid technical choices, strong people, and responsible practices—is how we win. 

This week I'm learning. I want to understand what's working, where we're stuck, and how I can help. Anything you think I should know?"**

---

**When to use:**
- Random hallway conversations
- Team all-hands (brief version)
- Informal lunch intro
- Anyone you'll meet casually

**Customizable parts:**
- `[background/previous role]` → e.g., "leading distributed systems teams at [company]" or "building cloud infrastructure at [company]"

## 90-Second Version
### (1:1 meeting, department intro, stakeholder conversation)

**Time:** ~90 seconds | **Tone:** Professional, thoughtful, genuine

---

**"Hi, I'm Robert Reilly, the new Team Lead for engineering. I come from [background], where I spent the last [X] years building and scaling engineering teams, focusing on three things: **technical excellence** — because the systems we build need to be rock-solid — **people** — because the strongest teams grow their own leaders — and **delivery** — because strategy means nothing without execution.

I joined Leidos because the mission matters. We're building systems that support critical decision-making for the nation, and that demands the best of us—technically, ethically, and culturally. I'm particularly interested in [one specific area: AI governance, DORA metrics, architecture patterns] because I think it's where we can have the most impact.

This is my first week, so I'm in learning mode. I'll be talking with everyone — engineers, product, operations, security — because I need to understand how things really work, what's frustrating the team, and where we have real opportunities. I'd love your perspective. What's going well right now? What's the biggest blocker you're facing?"**

---

**When to use:**
- 1:1s with direct reports
- Engineering or product leads
- Cross-functional stakeholders
- Department-level introductions

**Customizable parts:**
- `[background]` → Your detailed background
- `[X]` → Years of experience
- `[one specific area]` → Pick what's most relevant to the person's domain

## 5-Minute Version
### (Extended 1:1, executive conversation, deep stakeholder intro)

**Time:** ~5 minutes | **Tone:** Authentic, strategic, conversational

---

**Opening (45 seconds):**

"Hi, I'm Robert Reilly. I just started as Team Lead here, and I'm genuinely excited about the opportunity. Let me tell you a bit about where I'm coming from and what I see as the opportunity here.

I've spent the last [X] years in [industry/domain], leading engineering teams through [key challenge or accomplishment — e.g., 'scaling from 5 to 40 engineers', 'launching critical infrastructure', 'building responsible AI systems']. Along the way, I've learned that the best outcomes come from three things working together: 

**One, technical excellence.** We can't just ship code — we need to ship systems that are reliable, maintainable, and built on solid engineering principles. Architecture, testing, CI/CD, observability — these aren't bureaucracy, they're the foundation of delivery.

**Two, people and culture.** Strong teams don't happen by accident. They come from clarity about what we're trying to do, psychological safety to take smart risks, and real investment in each person's growth. The best leaders aren't gatekeepers — they're multipliers who make the people around them better.

**Three, delivery and impact.** All the code and people in the world don't matter if we don't ship things that move the needle. I'm focused on DORA metrics, delivery velocity, and reducing the gap between intent and outcome."

---

**About Leidos & This Role (60 seconds):**

"I joined Leidos because the mission resonates with me. The work you do here directly affects national security and critical decision-making. That's not abstract — it's real. And it means we have both a responsibility and an opportunity to do engineering *right*.

From what I'm learning, we have some real strengths: [pause for them to mention them, or gently offer] — I'm sure there are talented people here building important systems. At the same time, I suspect there are some common challenges: How do we scale without losing quality? How do we move fast while maintaining security and compliance? How do we invest in our people while delivering on aggressive timelines?

That's what I'm here to help solve. And honestly, I can't solve it alone — I need partners. I need people who care about the technical craft, who believe in building teams the right way, and who aren't afraid to say 'we need to do this differently.'"

---

**About AI & The Future (60 seconds):**

"One more thing I'm thinking about: AI is changing how we build software. And I think Leidos should be leading, not following, on how we use it responsibly. Not just 'can we use AI to move faster' — but 'how do we use AI in a way that makes sense for our mission, our teams, and our security posture?' That's a conversation I want to have early and often.

So here's what I'm asking for: **Tell me what matters to you.** What do you think we should be betting on? What frustrates you about how things work right now? Who do I need to meet? What would success look like in your eyes?

I'm here to listen first, learn second, and then help us move the needle on whatever matters most."

---

**Closing (15 seconds):**

"Thanks for taking time to meet. I'm genuinely excited about what we can build together. Let's find ways to work together."

---

**When to use:**
- 1:1 with your boss
- Extended conversations with key stakeholders
- Engineering director or VP-level intro
- When someone asks "tell me about yourself" in a deeper context
- Department or cross-team kickoff

**Customizable parts:**
- `[X]` → Years of experience
- `[industry/domain]` → Your background
- `[key challenge/accomplishment]` → Specific thing you led
- `[pause for them to mention/gently offer]` → Adapt based on their answer

## Tips for Tomorrow

### Context Clues
- **Your boss:** Use the 5-minute version, but listen more than you talk
- **Your team:** Use 30-second, then 90-second for 1:1s
- **Cross-functional (product/ops/security):** Use 90-second, adjust the "specific area" to what they care about
- **All-hands:** Use 30-second version, keep it under 1 minute

### Delivery Tips
1. **Pause and breathe** — Don't rush, especially in longer versions
2. **Make eye contact** — Shows confidence and genuine interest
3. **Smile** — Sets the tone for collaboration
4. **Listen after** — Ask questions, don't just deliver a speech
5. **Adapt on the fly** — If they interrupt with interest, lean into that
6. **Be authentic** — Use your own words, don't memorize word-for-word

### Red Flags to Avoid
- ❌ Don't criticize the previous leader ("things were broken")
- ❌ Don't make promises you're unsure about ("I'll fix that immediately")
- ❌ Don't get too detailed/technical on first intro
- ❌ Don't dominate the conversation (listen at least as much as you talk)
- ❌ Don't use jargon that assumes shared context

### Credibility Signals
- ✅ Specific examples (not vague claims)
- ✅ Acknowledging what came before ("building on what you've created")
- ✅ Asking good questions ("what frustrates you?")
- ✅ Humility about learning mode
- ✅ Genuine enthusiasm (not fake energy)
